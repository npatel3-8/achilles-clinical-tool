Fluoroquinolones (e.g. ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) are associated with increased risk of Achilles tendon rupture, particularly in older adults. NICE advises vigilance in patients on or recently completing fluoroquinolone therapy.
Systemic or locally injected corticosteroids weaken tendon structure and increase rupture risk. Inquire about recent steroid injections near the Achilles or prolonged oral corticosteroid therapy.
Pain at rest or that wakes the patient at night, unrelated to tendon loading, may suggest serious underlying pathology such as malignancy, infection, or inflammatory condition requiring further investigation.
Conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis, spondyloarthropathy, or gout may predispose to tendon pathology and warrant specialist rheumatological input alongside MSK management.
Bilateral presentation may indicate a systemic cause such as inflammatory arthropathy, medication side-effect (e.g. statins, fluoroquinolones), or metabolic condition. Further investigation is warranted.
Unintentional weight loss alongside musculoskeletal symptoms is a red flag for possible malignancy or serious systemic disease and warrants urgent further investigation.
Calf swelling and pain may mimic Achilles pathology but could indicate deep vein thrombosis. Consider Wells score assessment and urgent investigation if DVT is suspected.
A high-energy mechanism (e.g. fall from height, direct blow, road traffic accident) raises suspicion for fracture, complete rupture, or other significant structural injury requiring urgent imaging and specialist assessment.

    Achilles Tendinopathy — Evidence-Based Management

    Working diagnosis: Achilles Tendinopathy

    Phase 1: Pain Control (Weeks 0–4)

    • Isometric calf holds: 30–45 seconds, 4–5 reps, 2–3 times/day
    • Load modification — reduce provocative activities but avoid complete rest

    Phase 2: Strength (Weeks 4–12)

    • Heel raises: progress from double-leg → single-leg
    • 3–4 sets of 8–12 reps
    • Slow tempo: 3 seconds up, 3 seconds down

    Phase 3: Return to Sport (Week 12+)

    • Plyometrics: hopping, skipping, bounding
    • Sport-specific drills with graduated loading

    Key Advice

    • Avoid complete rest — tendons respond to progressive load
    • Avoid corticosteroid injections into the tendon
    • Imaging only if symptoms persist >3 months or diagnosis unclear

    Achilles Tendon Rupture — Management

    Working diagnosis: Achilles Tendon Rupture

    Immediate Management:

    • Urgent referral to A&E / Orthopaedics (same day)
    • Immobilise ankle in plantarflexion
    • Non-weight-bearing with crutches
    • No exercises — await specialist review
